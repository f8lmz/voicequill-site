The cheat sheet

The inline controls

Type these marks right into your script. They’re how you direct the read — where it breathes, what it leans on, how a word is said. Marks are removed from the audio; only their effect remains.

*your words*

Emphasis

Wrap text in single asterisks to lean on it — a small beat just before, plus a lift in stress. Use it on the word that carries the line. It’s a nudge, not a shout (the voice doesn’t get louder, it gets deliberate).

ExampleI *never* said that. → a beat, then weight on “never.”
~your words~

Soften

Wrap text in tildes to flatten it — stress is stripped, so it reads quicker and lighter. Good for throwaway asides or the quiet half of a contrast.

ExampleIt was fine, ~i guess~. → the aside slips by, underplayed.
[pause]

Short pause — ~0.35s

Drops real silence into the audio (not a guess — actual inserted quiet). Use it to let a point land or to separate two thoughts.

ExampleWait for it. [pause] There it is.
[pause:long]

Long pause — ~0.75s

A longer beat for suspense, sign-offs, or breathing room. (Anything after the colon works — [pause:long], [pause:beat] — it’s all the long pause.)

ExampleAnd the winner is… [pause:long] you.
[pause][pause] …stack them

Stack for bigger gaps

Pauses add up. Put two or three in a row for a dramatic hold that’s longer than a single long pause.

ExampleThree. [pause][pause] Two. [pause][pause] One.
[note](say-it-like-this)

Pronunciation override

The part in [ brackets ] is just a note to yourself — it isn’t spoken. The part in ( parentheses ) is what’s actually said. Spell it the way it sounds. Perfect for names, brands, and foreign words the voice would otherwise mangle.

ExampleOur founder, [Siobhan](shiv-awn), built this in Amsterdam. → says “shiv-awn.”
,   .   —   …

Punctuation is free rhythm

You don’t need a mark for every breath. Commas, periods, dashes (—) and ellipses (…) already shape the pacing. Three dots ... become a single trailing-off beat. Reach for [pause] when you want more than punctuation gives.

ExampleI thought about it — a lot — and honestly… I’m in.
See the difference

The same script, with and without

Plain text works fine. A few marks make it sound like someone meant it. Here’s the same copy, both ways.

A dramatic line (fiction / trailer)
Plain
The house was empty. She knew what came next. There was no turning back.
With marks
The house was empty. [pause] She knew what came next. *There was no turning back.*
Why it lands: the pause lets “empty” hang; the emphasis puts a beat and weight on the final line so it reads like a verdict, not a sentence.
An instruction (tutorial / demo)
Plain
Click Export. Choose WAV for the highest quality. That’s it.
With marks
Click *Export*. Choose *WAV* for the highest quality. That’s it — [pause] done.
Why it lands: emphasis flags the two things a viewer must not miss; the short pause before “done” gives the step a clean finish instead of trailing off.
A tricky name (branding / education)
Plain
Today’s recipe comes from Siobhan, who runs a bakery in Reading.
With marks
Today’s recipe comes from [Siobhan](shiv-awn), who runs a bakery in [Reading](redding).
Why it lands: left alone, both words get read wrong. The overrides make them say “shiv-awn” and “redding” — small fix, big credibility.
Good to know

A few pointers

Close your marks. * and ~ work in pairs — one to start, one to end. Forget the closing mark and the effect runs to the end of the line.
Less is more. Emphasize one word per line, not five. If everything’s stressed, nothing is.
Stack pauses for drama. One [pause] for a breath; [pause][pause] for a hold; [pause:long] for suspense.
Spell it like it sounds. In a pronunciation override, write the parentheses phonetically — (nyawki), not (gnocchi).
Preview, then commit. Generate a line, listen, adjust a mark, regenerate. It’s fast because it’s local.
Punctuation first. Get the commas and periods right before adding marks — they carry most of the rhythm on their own.
